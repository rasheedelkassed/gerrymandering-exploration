It's Not Arbitrary

An explanation of unexplainable electoral boundaries


By Rasheed El Kassed

Welcome to a fictitious country I'm calling the Grided Republic.

You've just been tasked with drawing the district boundaries for one of the states in the Grided Republic. A state in the Grided Republic is made up of neighborhoods that are each assigned a certain political party based on the voter base of said neighborhoods (the Purple and Orange political parties are the popular ones), and the districts must all contain exactly five connected neighborhoods:

The winning political party of a district dictates the political party of that district's representative, and ideally we would want perfect representation:

This seems completely fine! No matter how you decide to draw the two districts, there is no way to draw them in an unfair way. 50% of the neighborhoods side with the Purple party, and 50% of the representatives also side with the Purple party. The same is true with the Orange party.

Let's go ahead and increase the size of our state to something a tiny bit bigger and see what happens now. Let's also add some fun statistics to accompany our hypothetical state.

This still seems completely fine! Chances are the districts you just drew give the Orange party 2 representatives, and the Purple party 1 representative, a totally fair way to draw these districts as there are clearly more Orange aligned neighborhoods compared to Purple aligned ones.
