It's Not Arbitrary

An explanation of unexplainable electoral boundaries






PLAY →

In 1812, the state of Massachusetts was forced to adopt the idea of electoral district boundaries.

Funnily enough, also in 1812, the state governor of Massachusetts, Elbridge Gerry, signed a bill that drew the newly required electoral districts of Massachusetts in a way that favored his Democratic-Republican Party.

The new districts proved to be a huge success for the Democratic-Republican Party, helping secure the majority of control in the state's senate. What really sucks is that despite Elbridge Gerry sharing his disapproval of the underhanded tactic, he nevertheless became the namesake of what we now call Gerrymandering.

This, however, begs the question: How exactly does just drawing some borders on a map influence anything, let alone an election?

I think looking at pictures and diagrams is great and all, but I don't think it really sells it well enough. There is only one thing I think can really clear this up...


...playing a game!

How could that be?

The reason this is possible is due to something known as the wasted vote effect. If a district is split 3:2, it seems as though the 2 votes that were cast did not contribute towards electing any sort of representative and were completely wasted!

We can use this fact to dilute one parties voters over several districts, thus diminishing their voting power. Purposefully taking advantage of the wasted vote effect in this way is called cracking.



Let's try out cracking!

Here we have a state that favors Orange 3 to 2 (15 Orange neighborhoods to 10 Purple ones). Instead of keeping the ratio of representative equal to the ratio of voters, I want you to give Orange full control of all five possible districts. In short:

Have Orange win all districts. (Try and barely win the districts)

While cracking does a good job, it's not always possible to achieve 100% control of a state (in reality, it's pretty much impossible).

To get around this, we can use a different method along with cracking called packing.

Packing is the intentional stuffing of many votes in as concentrated a place as possible.

Let's try out packing.

Here we have a state where we have a fairly even split between the two colors. What I want you to do is:

Give Purple a fully winning district (as in, five Purple neighborhoods), and then have Orange win all other districts.

It is clear that these methods of political influence change the democratic dynamic from people picking their representatives to representatives picking their people. Plain and simple, elections are no longer competitive if these systems exist.

There are a number of systems that could be put in place to ensure a competitive elections. The first, and simplest, solution is to implement a neutral or cross-party agency responsible for drawing the districts. This agency would be selected from apolitcal individuals and would help ensure there was no political bias in the drawing of districts. On top of this, members of this agency would be denied information such as voting patterns to ensure any biases are unable to show themselves in any meaningful way.

There really is a whole host of possible solutions to this problem:

◘ Change the voting system in a way that gets rid of the wasted vote effect (e.g. ranked choice).

◘ Provide objective rules to guide the creation of districts, with a committee to uphold those rules.

◘ Require transparency through the use of regulation.
