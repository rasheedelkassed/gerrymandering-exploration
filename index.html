It's Not Arbitrary

An explanation of unexplainable electoral boundaries


By Rasheed El Kassed

In 1812, the state of Massachusetts was forced to adopt the idea of electoral district boundaries.

Also in 1812, the state governor of Massachusetts, Elbridge Gerry, signed a bill that drew the newly required electoral districts of Massachusetts in a way that favored his Democratic-Republican Party

The new districts proved to be a huge success for the Democratic-Republican Party, helping secure the majority of control in the state's senate. Funnily enough, our man Gerry ended up losing his job due to the over-whelming support for the Federalist party.

This begs the question: How exactly does just drawing some borders influence an election?

I think looking at pictures and diagrams is great and all, but I don't think it really sells it well enough. There is only one thing I think can really clear this up:


Playing a game!

A Game of Borders


You have a state in front of you that you have to draw the electoral districts for. In this case, it's being represented by a collection of neighborhoods on a square grid. Here are the rules:

◘ Click & Drag to draw a district.

◘ Click an existing district to delete it.

◘ Each district must contain exactly FIVE CONTINUOUS squares.

◘ Each district elects a representative on a simple majority system, meaning that a district with 3 Orange and 2 Purple neighborhoods elects an Orange sided representative.
